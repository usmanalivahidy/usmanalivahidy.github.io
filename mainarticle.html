FIVE OF THE WORLD'S MOST ENDANGERED ANIMALS

The IUCN Red List now contains 41,415 species, 16,306 of which are threatened with extinction as endangered species. Compared to previous year, this represents an increase of 16,118. Both endangered species of animals and plants fall under this category. Which of these creatures is most in danger of going extinct? Continue reading to find out.
1.JAVAN RHINOS

Javan rhinos were formerly widespread in south-east Asia, but habitat degradation and poaching have caused a shocking drop in their population. One of the rarest rhino species, Javan rhinos are only found in one natural population on the Indonesian island of Java, where there are only around 75 of them. The only surviving home for Javan rhinos is the Ujung Kulon National Park, which is a World Heritage Site. However, the region is also negatively impacted by the invasive Arenga palm, which gives the rhinos less food to consume and less space to wander. Additionally, they were exterminated because they were agricultural pests and because their horn was highly valued in ancient Asian medicine. The threat of poaching is still quite real.

2.AMUR LEOPARD
With only around 100 remaining in the wild, the Amur leopard is one of the most endangered big cats in the world. These leopard subspecies have been listed as severely endangered since 1996, despite the fact that their natural population appears to be stable and growing. And there's a solid reason for it: At the moment, Amur leopards are only known to exist in a very tiny area between north-eastern China and Russia's far east. Threats to the surviving Amur leopards' existence include habitat degradation and fragmentation, a lack of available prey, and infrastructure for moving people, such highways. For this unusual huge cat, there is yet hope. In Russia and China, protected areas make up around 75% of their native range, and they are also relocating to more appropriate habitats elsewhere.

3.SUNDA ISLAND TIGER
The Sumatran tiger, also known as the Sunda Island tiger, is the world's smallest tiger subspecies, weighing up to 140 kilograms.As a point of comparison, male Amur tigers can weigh up to twice as much as Sunda Island tigers, making them the largest of all big cats.They are also extremely uncommon; only the Indonesian island of Sumatra is home to approximately 600 of them in the wild.South-east Asia's human population has nearly doubled since the 1980s, from 357 million in 1980 to 668 million in 2020.Consequently, this affects tiger numbers, which have been contracting alongside their living spaces.Sunda Island tigers are more likely to come into contact with people as the number of human settlements in the area grows, which could lead to more human-tiger conflict.The illegal trade in tiger parts and products and the poaching of tigers are also serious threats to their survival.

4.MOUNTAIN GORILLA
The Mountain gorilla is a subspecies of the eastern gorilla. It lives in two isolated populations in Uganda's Bwindi Impenetrable National Park and the high-altitude forests up in the volcanic, mountainous regions of the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Rwanda, and Uganda.Along with high rates of poverty, the Virunga Landscape has a history of political instability.This poses a significant threat to the number of mountain gorillas because people have moved closer to these great apes for food, shelter, and space—at the moment, over 500,000 people live near their habitat.Despite this, the International Gorilla Conservation Programme of the WWF and conservation efforts and interventions by local and international partners are contributing to a promising recovery for mountain gorillas.With just over 1,000 wild individuals, mountain gorillas are currently listed as an endangered species.However, this species' recovery may be impeded by a number of threats.

5.TAPANULI ORANGUTAN
The Tapanuli orangutan is a newly discovered orangutan species that was recognised as a separate species in 2017. The tropical forests of the Batang Toru habitat on the Indonesian island of Sumatra are the only place where Tapanuli orangutans may be found in the wild. They are the most endangered great ape species in the world as there are currently less than 800 of these monkeys left in the wild. As tropical forests are replaced by agricultural, mining, and hydroelectric and geothermal development, habitat loss is one of the biggest dangers to its survival.The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) has listed it as critically endangered due to hunting, human conflict, illegal wildlife trade, widespread habitat destruction for small-scale agriculture, mining, and a proposed hydroelectric dam, the Batang Toru hydropower project, in the region with the highest density of orangutans, which could negatively affect up to 10% of its already diminishing habitat.
